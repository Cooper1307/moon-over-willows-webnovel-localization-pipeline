## Chapter 1: The Cyberbullying of a Web Novelist

The morning sun struggled to pierce the gaps in the curtains, casting a single sliver of light across the bedroom of a twenty-second-floor high-rise apartment.

The curtains were a deep purple, and the morning glare outside made the fabric glow, saturating the bedroom air with a faint, violet haze.

The room was decorated with a minimalist grace. There, Qianli Xun lay sprawled on the bed, snoring softly. Her messy hair was a dark fan against the pillow, the quilt pulled snug to her neck as she drifted in a deep, peaceful slumber.

Then, the bedside table erupted. A jaunty, chart-topping pop tune filled the room.

Without even slitting an eye, Qianli Xun blindly swatted at the table. After a few seconds of fumbled searching, she finally snagged the device. Still blind to the world, she croaked in a voice thick with sleep, "Hello? Who is this and what do you want?"

"Master! How are you still asleep?!" The voice of her apprentice, Ergang, crackled with frantic energy on the other end. "Get your phone open, now! Watch the video I sent—the entire internet is tearing you a new one!"

==Qianli Xun’s eyes snapped open. She sat bolt upright, sleep vanishing in an instant. "What? The whole internet is cursing me? Since when? Why?"==

"See for yourself! I told you a long time ago that writing web novels was a bad move, but did you listen? No! Do yourself a favor and stay inside for a few days. Goodbye!"

The call went dead.

Qianli Xun sat there for a moment, the quilt slipping down her shoulders to reveal a set of pale purple pajamas. She scrambled to open her chat app and found Ergang’s avatar—the God of Wealth, fitting for a guy whose only dream was striking it rich. She tapped the video link he’d sent and a woman’s voice poured out of the speakers:

"...Web novelist Qianli Xun has been called out by legions of netizens. Accusations are flying—plagiarism, self-plagiarism, and blatant word-padding in her latest works. Critics claim she’s lost the spark of her early days, descending into formulaic, uninspired drivel that no longer resonates with readers..."

Qianli Xun’s brow furrowed. She killed the video before it could finish, her blood beginning to boil. "Drivel? I’ve dominated the webnovel world for ten years!" she spat. "How I choose to write is none of your damn business!"

She tossed the phone aside, swung her legs out of bed, and shoved her feet into a pair of slippers. In the kitchen, she snagged a bottle of milk from the fridge and poured half a glass.

It was ice-cold—horrible for the digestion, as she well knew. But she was in no mood to wait for the microwave. She grabbed the thermos from the counter and topped off the glass with boiling water. The temperature was just right. She downed the lukewarm mix in three massive gulps and slammed the glass onto the table with a resounding *thwack*.

She paced through the living room and stepped onto the balcony, peering down at the street below. A swarm of reporters, microphones at the ready, were milling around the entrance, their heads craned upward like hungry birds. Thank god for high-end communities; the security in the lobby was tight enough to keep that rabble at bay.

A dry, mirthless smile touched her lips. "Well, look at that. I guess I really *am* famous. You haven't truly made it until you've been cyberbullied by the masses."

She retreated from the balcony, went back to the bedroom, and snatched the phone from the bed. Sitting on the edge of the mattress, she took a deep breath. "Alright. Let's see just how famous you've become."

She opened a short-video app. The very first clip was a roast—of her. It was a plain-looking streamer, her voice a slow, patronizing drawl:

"Hello, family! We all know the name Qianli Xun, right? Of course we do! She’s the 'famous' web novelist. I'm sure most of you have read her stuff—honestly, I used to be a fan too. Notice I said *used to*. Why did I stop? Because I grew up. I matured as a reader, but she? As a writer, she’s practically fossilized. She’s still churning out the same teenage fantasies for eighteen-year-olds. Is it any wonder her fans are jumping ship? Looking back at those old books, I can only think one thing: *God, you were so stupid and naive. How could you ever like this crap?*..."

Qianli Xun prided herself on her thick skin, but this was pushing it. She rounded on the screen, her voice sharp with a retort she knew the streamer couldn't hear. "What the hell do you know? Yours truly writes for a specific demographic! You liked them when you were a teenager, didn't you? That’s proof enough that I'm good at what I do! Don't blame me just because you got old and bitter!"

As she vented, a notification banner dropped from the top of the screen. She swiped away the video and opened the message. To her horror, more than half of her fan groups had dissolved.

"So what? Good riddance!" she barked. "Yours truly is an author, not some idol. I never wanted those damn fan groups in the first place—the site forced them on me. At least now things will be quiet. In fact, let’s just close the rest of them!"

Before she could act on the thought, another alert chimed. The remaining groups had plummeted into the single digits. Qianli Xun ignored it, swiped past the streamer, and was met with a bold, black heading: **"LITERATURE IS DEAD"**.

Intrigued, she paused. *Surely this one isn't about me,* she thought. *Might as well see what the high-brows are whining about.*

An elderly man appeared on screen, looking every bit the dusty academic. His voice was a booming, aggressive roar:

"Literature is dead! It is a tragedy! A travesty! The profound works of traditional masters gather dust, while the youth of today gorge themselves on the nutrient-free filth of web novels! Literature exists to serve reality! It must expose, critique, and illuminate! It must convey the ultimate Truth! Yet web novels, driven by nothing but greed, cater to the lowest common denominator. It is entertainment unto death! 'Face-slapping' tropes and amateurish scribblings are everywhere! Not one is fit to be called literature! Take, for instance, the works of Qianli Xun..."

The academic throat-clearing had been a setup. She'd become the poster child for the decline of civilization overnight. Well, fine. She’d listen to what the old fossil had to say.

"The entire internet is up in arms, so I forced myself to look at her 'novels.' I couldn't even finish a chapter. It is verbal sewage—utterly devoid of substance! Traditional literature possesses depth, history, and a richness that rewards the reader. Web novels? They are incomparable trash! I call on everyone! While the tide of public opinion is against her, let us rise up! Let us sweep away this web novel filth and usher in a true revival of traditional literature!..."

Qianli Xun jabbed at the screen, silencing the old man mid-rant. She shook her phone at the frozen image of his face.

*"Oh, you speak so passionately! If you're so damn brilliant, why don't you try writing a web novel? I guarantee you'd fail miserably! Listen up, old man: literature isn't dead. In the digital age, it has simply evolved to meet its audience. Have you ever even heard of literary history? If not, go pick up a book! Every era produces its own unique style. Traditional literature is a relic—it doesn't fit the modern world, and it was always destined to be phased out. Your 'revival' is a fantasy! Who knows? In a few hundred years, my work might be the new classic. And besides, what's so wrong with making people happy? Even Cao Xueqin said 'Dream of the Red Chamber' was just 'penciled for play and to suit one's temperament.' If the greatest of the Four Great Classics was written to entertain the masses, then yours truly is simply carrying on a proud tradition!..."* [TN: Cao Xueqin used the phrase "游戏笔墨，陶情适性" (playful writing, cultivating temperament) to describe the intent behind the masterpiece *Dream of the Red Chamber*.]

She was still mid-rant when the screen flickered. **"EDITOR YOUYOU"** pulsed in rhythmic green.

Qianli Xun hit 'accept'. A soft, apologetic female voice filled her ear. "Qianli Xun... site management has made a decision. Given the sheer scale of the online backlash, and to protect the platform's image, all of your works are being taken down. This includes your current serial. The site will provide appropriate compensation for the loss of income."

"They can't do that!" her voice cracked. "Being hated is just another form of fame! If anything, this is the time to keep them up—"

The editor didn't—or couldn't—listen. The line went dead.

Qianli Xun sat in the silence of her bedroom, the phone heavy in her hand. A wave of helplessness crashed over her, followed by a cold, sharp anger. She wanted to hurl the device against the wall, to hear it shatter, but she forced her fingers to loosen. She had to think. She had to find a way out. Was her career really over? Was this the end of the thing she loved most in the world?
